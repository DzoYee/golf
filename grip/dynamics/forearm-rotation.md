# Forearm Rotation

[← Dynamics](INDEX.md) · [← Grip](../INDEX.md)

Forearm rotation is the twisting motion of the left and right forearms through the swing. It's one of the primary mechanisms that squares the clubface to the target at impact. The [static grip](../statics/INDEX.md) determines how much forearm rotation is needed — the grip sets the "rotation budget."

## What Happens

During the backswing, the forearms rotate to open the clubface (the left forearm supinates, the right forearm pronates). During the downswing and through impact, the forearms rotate back the other way to square and then close the face (the left forearm pronates, the right forearm supinates).

The amount of this return rotation that's needed depends on where the hands started:

| Grip Strength | Rotation Needed | Why |
|--------------|----------------|-----|
| [Weak](../statics/weak.md) | High | Face starts open, so more rotation is required to square it by impact |
| [Neutral](../statics/neutral.md) | Moderate | Face starts near square, requiring a natural amount of rotation |
| [Strong](../statics/strong.md) | Low | Face starts closed or near-closed, so less rotation (or even active resistance) is needed |

This is the core relationship between statics and dynamics — the grip sets the rotation demand, and the body has to deliver it. See [statics → dynamics](../connections/statics-to-dynamics.md) for the full mapping.

## Rotation Rate vs. Timing

Two aspects of forearm rotation matter:

1. **Rotation rate** — How fast the forearms rotate. This is largely a physical characteristic of the golfer (some people rotate faster naturally).

2. **Rotation timing** — When in the downswing the rotation accelerates. Early rotation squares the face sooner; late rotation squares it later.

The grip affects the **demand** — how much total rotation is needed. The golfer's natural rotation rate and timing determine the **supply**. When supply matches demand, the face arrives square. When they don't match:

- Supply > demand → face closes → hooks, pulls → see [face control](face-control.md)
- Supply < demand → face stays open → fades, slices → see [face control](face-control.md)

## Grip Strength as a Rotation Dial

This is why grip strength is one of the most powerful variables in the swing. Instead of trying to change the golfer's natural forearm rotation (a motor pattern that's hard to alter), changing the grip changes the demand to match the supply:

- **Fast rotator slicing?** Unlikely — probably an open face from something else
- **Slow rotator slicing?** Strengthen the grip to reduce the rotation demand
- **Fast rotator hooking?** Weaken the grip to increase the rotation demand
- **Slow rotator hooking?** Unlikely — check path and face alignment

This is why many teachers start with grip when diagnosing ball flight: it's the most direct way to match face-closing demand to the golfer's natural rotation supply.

## Interaction with Both Hands

The left and right forearms rotate as a connected system, but they're not identical:

- The **[left hand](../statics/left-hand.md)** (lead hand) has more influence on the overall face angle because it's higher on the grip and more connected to the body's rotation through the [left shoulder](../connections/left-shoulder.md).
- The **[right hand](../statics/right-hand.md)** (trail hand) can add or override rotation through the hitting zone — an overactive right hand can flip the face closed regardless of the left hand's position.

When both hands are at the same strength ([matched combinations](../statics/combinations.md)), the forearms rotate as a unit. When they're at different strengths ([mismatched combinations](../statics/combinations.md)), the forearms may fight each other, creating inconsistency.

## Common Patterns

These are one way to interpret these symptoms — grip is often a reasonable place to look, but it's one of several variables. See [face squaring mechanisms](face-squaring-mechanisms.md) for the broader picture.

| Pattern | One Possible Interpretation | One Possible Grip Adjustment |
|---------|----------------------------|------------------------------|
| Consistent slice | Rotation supply may not be meeting demand | Strengthen left hand to reduce demand |
| Consistent hook | Rotation may be exceeding demand | Weaken left hand to increase demand |
| Inconsistent face angle (both hooks and slices) | Hands may be at conflicting strengths | Match the hand strengths to reduce internal conflict |
| Good face control but consistent directional miss | Rotation timing may be fine — path problem | Grip adjustment may not help here |
