# Strong Grip

[← Statics](INDEX.md) · [← Grip](../INDEX.md)

A strong grip means the hand is rotated away from the target on the club. Like [weak](weak.md), "strong" is a positional term — it describes rotation, not squeeze pressure. A grip can be strong in the [left hand](left-hand.md), the [right hand](right-hand.md), or both.

## What It Looks Like

**Left hand strong:**
- 3–4 knuckles visible at address (looking down)
- The V formed by the thumb and index finger points toward the right shoulder or beyond
- The left forearm is rotated inward (pronated more than neutral)

**Right hand strong:**
- The palm faces more toward the sky
- The V formed by the thumb and index finger points well right of the right shoulder
- The right hand sits more under the grip

## What It Does

A strong grip creates a starting position where the clubface is biased toward **closed**. The downstream effects:

1. **Less rotation needed to square the face** — Because the face is already pre-closed at address, the golfer needs less [forearm rotation](../dynamics/forearm-rotation.md) through the downswing to square it at impact. This reduces timing demand.

2. **Draw/hook tendency** — If the golfer maintains their normal rotation, the face arrives at impact closed, producing right-to-left curvature (draw or hook). See [face control](../dynamics/face-control.md).

3. **Lower ball flight** — A closed face reduces dynamic loft at impact, producing lower launch with less spin.

## When It's Used

- Players who slice the ball often move to a stronger grip to reduce the open face at impact
- Players who want to hit a draw as their stock shot
- Many modern tour players play with a moderately strong left hand grip
- Players with slower forearm rotation who need the grip to do some of the closing work

## Tradeoffs

| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|--------------|
| Reduces slice tendency | Increases hook tendency if rotation isn't reduced |
| Less timing-dependent — face is pre-set closer to square | Can produce low, hooking shots if overdone |
| Generally adds distance (lower spin, stronger launch) | Makes it harder to hit a fade when needed |
| More forgiving of slow forearm rotation | Very strong grips can restrict wrist mobility |

## The "Both Strong" Extreme

When both hands are strong — the [strong-strong combination](combinations.md) — the face is heavily biased closed. This requires the golfer to either:
- Actively hold off the release (resist forearm rotation) to prevent hooks
- Aim right and play a draw as the stock shot
- Accept that the miss will be a low hook

This is a high-bias grip: it solves the open-face problem aggressively but creates a new problem on the other side. See the [combinations matrix](combinations.md) for how matching and mismatching strengths changes the picture.
