# Left Hand Grip

[← Statics](INDEX.md) · [← Grip](../INDEX.md)

The left hand (for a right-handed golfer) is the lead hand. It's the primary connection between the club and the body's rotational engine. How the left hand sits on the club has downstream effects on [face angle](../dynamics/face-control.md), [forearm rotation](../dynamics/forearm-rotation.md), and the [left shoulder's position](../connections/left-shoulder.md).

## Placement: Fingers vs. Palm

Where the club sits across the left hand — how much in the fingers versus the palm — is an area of genuine debate in instruction. There are two broad camps:

**Traditional finger-grip position:** The club runs diagonally across the fingers, from roughly the base of the index finger toward the first joint of the pinkie. In this position, the club doesn't run through the middle of the palm. Many traditional instructors (Hogan, Penick and others) emphasize this position on the grounds that:
- It allows for a fuller range of wrist hinge
- The fingers provide more feel and sensitivity
- The club is less likely to be "locked" by the palm

**More palm-involved position:** Some modern instruction, particularly from biomechanics-oriented teachers, argues that having the club seat more into the palm can actually facilitate certain rotation mechanics. The reasoning varies by instructor, but one argument is that a more palm-involved grip changes the forearm's starting position in a way that may be advantageous for specific swing styles — particularly those that use body rotation rather than active forearm rotation to square the face. This is an area of active exploration without clear consensus.

In practice, most grips sit somewhere on a spectrum between these two extremes. The distinction between "finger grip" and "palm grip" is partly one of degree.

## Heel Pad and Hand Structure

Most instruction agrees that the fleshy pad at the base of the left palm should sit on top of the grip — this provides a natural downward pressure point. What varies is how much of the rest of the hand sits above vs. below the club.

## Thumb Position

How far the left thumb extends down the shaft — the long thumb vs. short thumb question — is a related variable. See [thumb position](thumb-position.md) for that discussion, as it interacts with where the club sits in the hand.

## Knuckle Visibility and Grip Strength

The number of knuckles visible when you look down at address is a common way to describe left hand grip strength:

| Knuckles Visible | Grip Strength | Link |
|------------------|--------------|------|
| 0–1 | [Weak](weak.md) | Hand rotated toward the target |
| 2 | [Neutral](neutral.md) | Baseline position |
| 3–4 | [Strong](strong.md) | Hand rotated away from the target |

This knuckle count is a proxy — what it's measuring is how the hand is rotated on the club, which affects the forearm's starting position and therefore how much [forearm rotation](../dynamics/forearm-rotation.md) is needed to square the face through the swing.

## Role in the Swing

Two main jobs attributed to the left hand:

1. **Sets the face angle baseline** — the rotational position of the left hand pre-sets how much the face is open or closed before motion begins. This is the "static contribution" to [face control](../dynamics/face-control.md).

2. **Connects to the left shoulder** — the left hand, wrist, and forearm form a chain to the [left shoulder](../connections/left-shoulder.md). The hand's rotation on the club tends to shift the shoulder's orientation, which can influence swing plane and path.

## Common Discussion Points

These are areas where instruction often focuses, though views on each vary:

- **Grip in the palm vs. fingers** — covered above; both have advocates with different reasoning
- **Thumb length** — see [thumb position](thumb-position.md)
- **Squeeze in the index finger and thumb vs. last three fingers** — many instructors cue pressure primarily in the last three fingers and the heel pad, arguing that index-finger/thumb pressure creates tension in the forearm that restricts rotation. This is widely taught but not universal.
- **Wrist tension** — how much the left wrist is set vs. relaxed at address feeds into what's possible dynamically. This connects to [wrist conditions at impact](../dynamics/face-squaring-mechanisms.md) later in the swing.
