# Left Hand Grip

[← Statics](INDEX.md) · [← Grip](../INDEX.md)

The left hand (for a right-handed golfer) is the lead hand. It's the primary connection between the club and the body's rotational engine. Where the left hand sits on the club determines the baseline face angle and establishes the relationship between the left arm and [left shoulder](../connections/left-shoulder.md).

## Placement

The club runs diagonally across the fingers of the left hand, from the base of the index finger to just below the pad of the palm. This is a **finger grip**, not a palm grip — the club should not run straight through the middle of the palm.

**Key checkpoints:**

- **Heel pad on top** — The fleshy pad at the base of the palm sits on top of the grip, providing downward pressure and control
- **Last three fingers** — The middle, ring, and pinkie fingers do most of the holding. These wrap under the grip and provide security
- **Index finger** — More of a trigger position, lightly curled. It doesn't squeeze hard
- **Thumb** — Sits slightly right of center on top of the grip (the "short thumb" position). It does not wrap down the side

## Knuckle Visibility

The number of knuckles visible when you look down at address is the standard way to gauge left hand grip strength:

| Knuckles Visible | Grip Strength | Link |
|-------------------|--------------|------|
| 0–1 | [Weak](weak.md) | Hand rotated toward the target |
| 2 | [Neutral](neutral.md) | Standard baseline position |
| 3–4 | [Strong](strong.md) | Hand rotated away from the target |

This knuckle count is the most common shorthand, but it's a proxy — what actually matters is how the hand's rotation on the club changes the forearm's starting position, which affects [forearm rotation](../dynamics/forearm-rotation.md) through the swing.

## Role in the Swing

The left hand does two jobs:

1. **Sets the face angle baseline** — The rotational position of the left hand on the club pre-sets how much the face is open or closed before the swing starts. This directly feeds into [face control](../dynamics/face-control.md) at impact.

2. **Connects to the left shoulder** — The left hand, wrist, and forearm form a mechanical chain to the [left shoulder](../connections/left-shoulder.md). Changing left hand position subtly shifts shoulder alignment and swing plane.

## Common Errors

- **Palm grip** — Club runs through the palm instead of the fingers. Reduces wrist hinge, costs distance, and makes face control harder.
- **Thumb too long** — Thumb extends far down the shaft. Reduces control and invites the right hand to dominate.
- **Squeezing with index finger and thumb** — Creates tension in the forearm that restricts [forearm rotation](../dynamics/forearm-rotation.md).
