# Thumb Position

[← Statics](INDEX.md) · [← Grip](../INDEX.md)

The position of the left (lead hand) thumb on the grip is a specific variable within the broader [left hand placement](left-hand.md) discussion. It affects how much of the grip runs through the fingers versus the palm, which in turn influences wrist hinge range, feel, and how the hand delivers the club.

## The Two Positions

**Short thumb:** The thumb doesn't extend far down the shaft. The top joint sits relatively high on the grip, near the top of the handle. The pad at the base of the thumb presses more prominently on top of the grip.

**Long thumb:** The thumb extends further down the shaft. The tip of the thumb sits lower on the grip. The club lies more fully across the fingers rather than riding up against the palm pad.

## Arguments for Each

### Arguments for Short Thumb

- Many traditional instructors advocate for the short thumb on the grounds that it creates a more secure, unified grip where the palm pad actively stabilizes the club
- The short thumb may reduce the tendency for the right hand to "take over" by crowding up the grip — with the left thumb shorter, the right hand has more room to settle naturally
- Some argue the short thumb produces a more repeatable feel because the reference point (thumb pad on top of grip) is more tangible than fingertip pressure
- Hogan and other mid-century instructors specifically cued "short thumb" as a fundamental checkpoint

### Arguments for Long Thumb

- A longer thumb position places the club more fully in the fingers, which some argue allows for a greater range of wrist hinge
- More wrist hinge range may facilitate more lag retention and a later release, which some biomechanically-oriented instructors consider advantageous
- In certain grip strength configurations, particularly more neutral-to-strong grips, a longer thumb position may change how the forearm and wrist can rotate — an area of active exploration in modern instruction
- Players with longer fingers may find a short thumb position unnatural or overly restrictive

## Connection to the Fingers vs. Palm Debate

Thumb position is part of the broader question of how much the left hand grip is a "finger grip" versus a "palm grip" — see [left hand placement](left-hand.md) for that discussion. A short thumb tends to increase palm involvement; a long thumb tends to shift contact toward the fingers. These aren't fully independent variables.

## What's Unresolved

There's no strong consensus on which thumb position is universally better. The debate tends to be filtered through the teacher's broader grip philosophy:

- Teachers who emphasize a unified, stable hand unit tend to prefer the short thumb
- Teachers who emphasize wrist mobility and hinge tend to be more open to longer thumb positions
- Players may find one position simply more comfortable based on hand size and anatomy

This is probably an area where individual fit matters more than universal prescription.
