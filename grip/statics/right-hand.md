# Right Hand Grip

[← Statics](INDEX.md) · [← Grip](../INDEX.md)

The right hand (for a right-handed golfer) is the trail hand. It provides feel, speed, and fine-tuned control of the clubface. Where it sits on the club affects how it works with or against the [left hand](left-hand.md) through the swing.

## Placement

The right hand sits below the left on the grip. The club lies along the base of the fingers — even more so than the left hand, this is a finger grip.

**Key checkpoints:**

- **Lifeline covers the left thumb** — The fleshy crease of the right palm fits snugly over the left thumb. This is the main physical connection between the two hands and should feel like they lock together as a unit.
- **Middle two fingers** — The middle and ring fingers of the right hand do most of the work, wrapping under the grip
- **Index finger** — Curled in a trigger position, separated slightly from the middle finger. This finger provides feel and directional control
- **Pinkie finger** — Interlocks with (interlock grip), overlaps (overlap/Vardon grip), or sits alongside (ten-finger grip) the left hand's index finger
- **Thumb** — Sits slightly left of center on the grip, angled. It does not press straight down

## Grip Styles

The three standard ways the right pinkie connects to the left hand:

| Style | Description | Common Use |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| **Overlap (Vardon)** | Right pinkie rests on top of the gap between left index and middle fingers | Most common on tour |
| **Interlock** | Right pinkie interlocks with left index finger | Popular with smaller hands |
| **Ten-finger** | All ten fingers on the grip, right pinkie sits next to left index | More leverage, less unity |

The style doesn't change grip strength — it changes how unified the hands feel and how much independent action each hand has.

## Right Hand Strength

Like the left hand, right hand strength refers to rotation, not squeeze pressure:

| Position | Grip Strength | Link |
|----------|--------------|------|
| Palm faces more toward sky | [Strong](strong.md) | Hand rotated under the club |
| Palm faces the target | [Neutral](neutral.md) | Standard baseline |
| Palm faces more toward ground | [Weak](weak.md) | Hand rotated on top of the club |

The right hand strength is assessed independently from the left. See [combinations](combinations.md) for how they interact.

## Role in the Swing

The right hand has three jobs:

1. **Speed and release** — The right hand provides much of the hitting action through impact. It fires the club through the ball and controls the release of wrist angles.

2. **Face fine-tuning** — While the left hand sets the baseline face angle, the right hand makes micro-adjustments through impact. A right hand that's too active can override the left hand's position, making [face control](../dynamics/face-control.md) inconsistent.

3. **Path influence** — The right hand and right shoulder are connected. An overly dominant right hand can push the swing path over the top (out to in), affecting both direction and [low point control](../dynamics/low-point-control.md).

## Common Errors

- **Right hand too much on top** — Creates a weak right hand that wants to leave the face open. The golfer compensates by flipping the hands through impact.
- **Right hand too much under** — Creates an overly strong right hand that closes the face aggressively. Can produce hooks and low pulls.
- **Death grip with the right hand** — Squeezing too hard restricts the natural release of the club and reduces clubhead speed.
- **Lifeline not covering left thumb** — The hands feel disconnected, leading to inconsistent face control because the two hands move independently.
